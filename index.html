The mental shift

From reacting to directives, to owning what happens before it happens

As an assistant director, it made sense to wait for direction. As the director, waiting is the one thing you can no longer afford.

WHO YOU WERE

  • You took direction from someone who saw the whole program.
  • You could respond well to what landed in front of you.
  • Someone else was thinking about next week, next season, next year.
  • If something slipped, there was a director behind you to catch it.
  • Compliance, renewals, and licensing were things you supported, not owned.

WHO YOU ARE NOW

  • You see the whole program. No one is above you catching what you miss.
  • You have to be ahead of what's coming, not just responsive to what's in front of you.
  • You're the one thinking about next week, next season, next year — for everyone.
  • "I got busy" and "I didn't know" are no longer available to you.
  • DHS compliance, STARS renewal, and licensing are fully yours to plan, track, and prove.

The metaphor to hold onto

You don't play an instrument. You're the conductor.

A conductor isn't the best violinist in the room. Their job is knowing every part well enough to cue it before it's needed — even the section that isn't watching.

Classroom Teams
You know which room is about to struggle with a ratio gap before a teacher calls out — because you already looked at next week's schedule.
Families
You know the announcement about picture day needs to go out Thursday, not the morning of — because you're thinking two weeks ahead of the calendar, not two hours.
Compliance
You know the DHS certificate renews in 90 days and the STARS evidence is due this month — without anyone reminding you. That reminder used to come from someone else. Now it comes from you, to you.
Facilities
You notice the outlet cover that's missing on your morning walkthrough, not after an incident report is filed.
The violinist who's looking down at their music still comes in on time — because the conductor already knew the entrance was coming and cued them. That's the job now. Not playing every instrument. Knowing the whole score well enough that nothing catches you by surprise.

Why this is happening now

The Executive Director is stepping fully into that seat

That means real things move from shared to fully yours. Not because you're being left alone — because you're being trusted to own your site.

Health & safety, day to day

Continuous DHS-standard monitoring of your site is no longer something you flag up. It's something you run.

DHS license renewal

Knowing the deadline, starting the paperwork early, and making sure it's compliant well before it's due is your timeline to manage.

Keystone STARS renewal

Evidence, documentation, and readiness for STARS 4 live on your calendar now — planned for months out, not scrambled for in the final weeks.

You're the owner of your site

Not a manager executing someone else's checklist. The person who notices what isn't on the calendar yet — and prepares for it anyway.

Making the mindset operational

Your new operating rhythm

You can't fill your days with tasks and call it leadership. Being the director means protecting time to think ahead — and building the habits that make sure nothing slips through.

Weekly

Your calendar is your command center

  • Block time at the start of every week to look ahead — not just at what's due, but what season the center is in and what that usually brings.
  • Take a real clarity break: step back from the inbox and ask, "What's not on the calendar yet that I know is coming?"
  • Leave space on purpose. Something unplanned will happen every week — that's not an exception, that's the job.

Theme Days

Give recurring ownership a fixed day

  • A day for finances — expenses, budget lines, anything trending the wrong way.
  • A day for staff time cards and payroll accuracy.
  • A day for compliance — DHS, STARS, licensing status, upcoming deadlines.

Daily — Morning Tour

You're present before you're anywhere else

Walk every classroom — be seen, not just present in the building.
Check in with staff — one specific, genuine highlight per room.
Scan for compliance as you walk — outlet covers, exits, hazards.
Step in and support where a room needs it — don't just observe.
Be visible again at drop-off and pickup — not behind a desk.

The center doesn't run because you filled your day.
It runs because you were watching everything, ahead of time — before it needed you to.
